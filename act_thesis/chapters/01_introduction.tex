\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}
When H.K. Onnes first liquified helium in 1908 \cite{onnesLiquefactionHelium1991} he opened a temperature regime in which matter was found to behave in unexpected ways. Most famously it triggered the discovery of vanishing electrical resistance of mercury \cite{onnesMeasurementKnowledge1990}. The fact that the refrigerant itself possesses properties hugely different to those observed in normal liquid became apparent only gradually. First signs that exposed that something is happening when cooling down helium where measured thermodynamic irregularities. Density passing through a maximum at $2\,\mathrm{K}$ and calorimetry showing anomalously large specific-heat values \cite{onnesMeasurementKnowledge1990} are the beginning of still ongoing research of superfluidity. In 1927, Keesom and Wolfke recognized that these anomalies mark a phase boundary and introduced the names helium I and helium II for the two liquid states \cite{keesomTWODIFFERENTLIQUID1971}. More precise measurements of the specific heat showed a $\lambda$-shaped curve with a sharp maximum and no latent heat, yielding the name $\lambda$-transition point \cite{keesomNewMeasurementsSpecific1935a}. This observation motivated Ehrenfest's classification of phase transitions and Landau's phase transition theory \cite{terhaar17THEORYSPECIFIC1965}. 

The next big step in history of superfluidity where the two experiments conducted by Kapitzka \cite{kapitzaViscosityLiquidHelium1938} and Allen \& Misener \cite{allenFlowLiquidHelium1938}. Forcing helium through a gap of about half a micron between to polished disks or through fine capillaries yielded observations of vanishing viscosity. At this point the $\lambda$-transition had been known for a decade, what was new was the fact that the helium II phase can carry mass with essentially no dissipation. Over the next 20 years multiple theories were developed to explain that behavior. London supplied the first interpretation almost immediately, noting that \helium atoms are bosons and that the Bose-Einstein condensation temperature of an ideal gas at the density of liquid helium falls close to the $\lambda$-transition \cite{londonLPhenomenonLiquidHelium1938}. Tisza picked up on this idea and turned it into a transport theory by treating helium II as composed of two non-interacting fluids. He modeled one condensed component carrying no entropy, moving without viscosity and a thermally excited normal component behaving conventionally \cite{tiszaTransportPhenomenaHelium1938,tiszaTheorieLiquidesQuantiques1940}. The main breakthrough was delivered by Landau in 1941. He stuck with the two fluid organization of the liquid but rejected the parts of Tisza's work that categorized single atoms into the two components. Following Landau, in a strongly interacting liquid one has to describe interactions with the system through collective excitations. This idea is what paved the way for him to find the dispersion curve of superfluid helium II, where for low momenta phonons dominate the excitation spectrum and for higher moment the curve has a minimum corresponding to the so called "rotons". From this followed that dissipation requires the creation of an excitation carrying the flow's energy and momentum, and this is kinematically forbidden below a critical velocity fixed by the minimum of the dispersion curve $\varepsilon(p)/p$. Superfluidity was thereby explained not by assuming that one component has zero viscosity, but by showing that sufficiently slow flow has no excitation into which it can decay. The predicted curve was confirmed by neutron scattering measurements \cite{henshawModesAtomicMotions1961}.

In one sense then superfluidity is a solved problem. Yet the criterion does not itself predict when the liquid start absorbing energy and momentum, because the creation of bulk excitations is only one of the available channels. Experiments often show dissipation way below the critical landau velocity\cite{mcclintockIonsSuperfluidHelium1984}. In his 1955 paper \cite{feynmanChapterIIApplication1955}, Feynman proposed vortices as the alternative channel. In bulk helium II the dissipative dynamics are hard to predict from first principles: the critical velocity is not an intrinsic property of the liquid but is, among other things, fixed by where vortices nucleate at the container walls. Predicting the interaction of an object with its surrounding turns out to be quite a difficult task. Finite size systems like helium nanodroplets give a platform to research the fundamental mechanisms of superfluidity with the benefit of testing theories that are calculational feasible. Rotating molecules doped in these droplets, i.e. their rotational spectra, provide us with a nanoscale probe of energy and momentum absorption in such liquid. 

\section{State of the Art}
\label{sec:state-of-the-art}
\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.5\textwidth}

    \caption{Linewidths of different molecules plotted against their rotational energy. The blacked filled graph displaying the density of states of the elementary excitations (explained in detail in \ref{sec:superfluidity}). \cite{choiInfraredSpectroscopyHelium2006a}}
    \label{fig:ir}
\end{wrapfigure}
Grebenev first started measuring IR-rotational spectra of single \gls{ocs} molecules in helium I and  II \cite{grebenevSuperfluiditySmallHelium41998,toenniesSuperfluidHeliumDroplets2004a}. He observed sharp rotational lines in the superfluid case and a smeared out peak, indicating dissipation, when viscosity is present. Following this, researchers conducted experiments on rotational linewidths/ the lifetime of an excited state, to infer a connection between state energies and dissipation in the superfluid. It was found that an increase in linewidth tends to correlate with the number of states of elementary excitations at a given rotational energy (see Fig. \ref{fig:ir}). Most of the datapoints in this figure display different molecules, owing to the nature of their measurement procedure. Due to IR-spectroscopy being a one photon process and molecules at droplet temperatures (around $0.4\, \mathrm{K}$) occupying mostly in their rotational ground state, it is only possible to probe energy levels close to the thermal equilibrium distribution. This restricts the energies one can probe with a single type of molecule, hence explaining why many different molecules where needed to display a correlation between energy and dissipation. Changing the system to scan across an energy rangy, i.e. changing the molecule, introduces uncertainty. Every molecule is interacting differently with its environment, therefor it might be more intuitive to spin up one type of molecule to higher rotational states and measure the implied state lifetimes.

To address that problem, time-resolved techniques have been developed. The Stapelfeldt group has pursued this through laser-induced nonadiabatic (impulsive) alignment \cite{chatterleyRotationalCoherenceSpectroscopy2020,pentlehnerImpulsiveLaserInduced2013,cherepanovExcitedRotationalStates2021}. A moderately intense, nonresonant, linearly polarized laser pulse acts on the molecular polarizability anisotropy and impulsively sets the embedded molecule rotating, an interaction often referred to as "kicking" the molecule (explained in detail in sections \ref{sec:molecule_interaction}, \ref{sec:rotating_molecules}). The kick creates a broad superposition of rotational states evolving in time which is analyzed by using a pump–probe scheme. After a variable delay, a second, intense probe pulse Coulomb explodes the molecule, and scanning the delay maps out the rotational dynamics in the time domain. Crucially, the amount of angular momentum deposited scales with the intensity of the kick pulse, so that a stronger pulse populates progressively higher rotational states and thereby grants access to levels far above those reachable by one-photon IR spectroscopy. This spin-up comes at a price, however. Because the kick deposits angular momentum in a single sudden burst, it populates many rotational states at once, all of which evolve and decay simultaneously. As a result, the loss of coherence cannot be cleanly separated from the relaxation of rotational energy, and the lifetime of any individual rotational state remains inaccessible. What one would like instead is a way to deposit population into a well-defined rotational state, so that the dynamics of a specific level can be followed on its own.
\section{Objectives and Outline}
\label{sec:outline}
An optical centrifuge is a linearly polarized laser pulse whose polarization vector rotates about the propagation axis; a molecule whose most polarizable axis is captured by the field is dragged along and forced to follow this rotation. In previous work \cite{macphail-bartleyControlMolecularRotation2026a} it was demonstrated that molecules embedded in helium nanodroplets can indeed be made to follow such a field, using a \emph{constant-frequency} centrifuge that spins at a fixed, tunable rate. That experiment established that the centrifuge concept survives the transition into the superfluid environment, but a centrifuge rotating at constant frequency does not climb the rotational ladder. It drives, at most, a single resonant transition rather than progressively accelerating the molecule into ever higher rotational states.
To actually spin a molecule up, the centrifuge must accelerate. An accelerating field can, in principle, sweep the molecule sequentially through the rotational ladder (see section \ref{sec:rot_ladder}) and leave it in a narrow distribution centered at a high rotational state. This is the route toward the objective motivated above: preparing a single molecular species in a chosen high rotational state and following its relaxation directly, so that decoherence and energy-relaxation timescales can be disentangled and state lifetimes studied on a state-by-state basis.
Before that goal can be pursued, however, it must first be understood what an accelerating centrifuge actually does to a molecule embedded in a helium nanodroplet. Whether the molecule remains captured as the field accelerates, how efficiently angular momentum is transferred, and what limits the process imposes. Establishing this is the objective of the present work.
