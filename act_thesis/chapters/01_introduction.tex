\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}
When H.K. Onnes first liquified helium in 1908 \cite{onnesLiquefactionHelium1991} he opened a temperature regime in which matter was found to behave in unexpected ways. Most famously it triggered the discovery of vanishing electrical resistance of mercury \cite{onnesMeasurementKnowledge1990}. The fact that the refrigerant itself possesses properties hugely different to those observed in normal liquid became apparent only gradually. First signs that exposed that something is happening when cooling down helium where measured thermodynamic irregularities. Density passing through a maximum at $2\,\mathrm{K}$ and calorimetry showing anomalously large specific-heat values \cite{onnesMeasurementKnowledge1990} are the beginning of still ongoing research of superfluidity. In 1927, Keesom and Wolfke recognized that these anomalies mark a phase boundary and introduced the names helium I and helium II for the two liquid states \cite{keesomTWODIFFERENTLIQUID1971}. More precise measurements of the specific heat showed a $\lambda$-shaped curve with a sharp maximum and no latent heat, yielding the name $\lambda$-transition point \cite{keesomNewMeasurementsSpecific1935a}. This observation motivated Ehrenfest's classification of phase transitions and Landau's phase transition theory \cite{terhaar17THEORYSPECIFIC1965}. 

The next big step in history of superfluidity where the two experiments conducted by Kapitzka \cite{kapitzaViscosityLiquidHelium1938} and Allen \& Misener \cite{allenFlowLiquidHelium1938}. Forcing helium through a gap of about half a micron between to polished disks or through fine capillaries yielded observations of vanishing viscosity. At this point the $\lambda$-transition had been known for a decade, what was new was the fact that the helium II phase can carry mass with essentially no dissipation. Over the next 20 years multiple theories were developed to explain that behavior. London supplied the first interpretation almost immediately, noting that \helium atoms are bosons and that the Bose-Einstein condensation temperature of an ideal gas at the density of liquid helium falls close to the $\lambda$-transition \cite{londonLPhenomenonLiquidHelium1938}. Tisza picked up on this idea and turned it into a transport theory by treating helium II as composed of two non-interacting fluids. He modeled one condensed component carrying no entropy, moving without viscosity and a thermally excited normal component behaving conventionally \cite{tiszaTransportPhenomenaHelium1938,tiszaTheorieLiquidesQuantiques1940}. The main breakthrough was delivered by Landau in 1941. He stuck with the two fluid organization of the liquid but rejected the parts of Tisza's work that categorized single atoms into the two components. Following Landau, in a strongly interacting liquid one has to describe interactions with the system through collective excitations. This idea is what paved the way for him to find the dispersion curve of superfluid helium II, where for low momenta phonons dominate the excitation spectrum and for higher moment the curve has a minimum corresponding to the so called "rotons". From this followed that dissipation requires the creation of an excitation carrying the flow's energy and momentum, and this is kinematically forbidden below a critical velocity fixed by the minimum of the dispersion curve $\varepsilon(p)/p$. Superfluidity was thereby explained not by assuming that one component has zero viscosity, but by showing that sufficiently slow flow has no excitation into which it can decay. The predicted curve was confirmed by neutron scattering measurements \cite{henshawModesAtomicMotions1961}.

In one sense then superfluidity is a solved problem. Yet the criterion does not itself predict when the liquid start absorbing energy and momentum, because the creation of bulk excitations is only one of the available channels. Experiments often show dissipation way below the critical landau velocity\cite{mcclintockIonsSuperfluidHelium1984}. In his 1955 paper \cite{feynmanChapterIIApplication1955}, Feynman proposed vortices as the alternative channel. In bulk helium II the dissipative dynamics are hard to predict from first principles: the critical velocity is not an intrinsic property of the liquid but is, among other things, fixed by where vortices nucleate at the container walls. Predicting the interaction of an object with its surrounding turns out to be quite a difficult task. Finite size systems like helium nanodroplets give a platform to research the fundamental mechanisms of superfluidity with the benefit of testing theories that are calculational feasible. Rotating molecules doped in these droplets, i.e. their rotational spectra, provide us with a nanoscale probe of energy and momentum absorption in such liquid.

\label{sec:state-of-the-art}

Grebenev first started measuring IR-rotational spectra of single \gls{ocs} molecules in helium I and  II \cite{grebenevSuperfluiditySmallHelium41998,toenniesSuperfluidHeliumDroplets2004a}. He observed sharp rotational lines in the superfluid case and a smeared out peak, indicating dissipation, when viscosity is present. Following this, researchers conducted experiments on rotational linewidths/ the lifetime of an excited state, to infer a connection between state energies and dissipation in the superfluid. It was found that an increase in linewidth tends to correlate with the number of states of elementary excitations at a given rotational energy (see Fig. \ref{fig:ir}). Most of the datapoints in this figure display different molecules, owing to the nature of their measurement procedure. Due to IR-spectroscopy being a one photon process and molecules at droplet temperatures (around $0.4\, \mathrm{K}$) occupying mostly in their rotational ground state, it is only possible to probe energy levels close to the thermal equilibrium distribution. This restricts the energies one can probe with a single type of molecule, hence explaining why many different molecules where needed to display the correlation between energy and dissipation.
